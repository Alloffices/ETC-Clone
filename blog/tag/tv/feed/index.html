]]> It is officially summertime!  As the weather gets warmer the backyard becomes a destination for entertaining, grilling and relaxing.

Home builders have seen a growing trend in “outdoor living,” not just for entertaining, but for every-day living.  People want to use their backyard as another gathering place where the full home entertainment experience is brought outside, including televisions and audio systems set up in the backyard as an “outdoor living room.”

But just as you need outdoor furniture that will stand up to the weather, you will also need the right kind TV to perform in outdoor elements.

Outdoor TVs

Specialty outdoor TVs are designed to withstand all kinds of weather including rain, humidity, bugs, dust, and the varying high temperatures of summer – most of which a standard TV wouldn’t survive.  Two outdoor TV brands we recommend are SunBrite and Seura.  The sets come in larger sizes and both companies produce a quality product.  If you want to see what one of these TV’s can handle, here is an in-depth review from a reporter who tested a SunBrite TV at home.

Placing the TV

When assessing the placement of the outdoor TV we have two main concerns, glare from the sun and placement of power and HDMI cables.

Ideally the TV should be placed in an area that has a little covering to cut down on the direct light from the sun, but it is not required.  These TVs are calibrated differently from a regular TV so you can see an amazing picture with the extra light; a covering will cut down on the amount of glare reflected off the TV.

Cabling is our second concern. If you are building a home, it is important to have the proper cabling placed in advance to provide high-quality access for audio and video entertainment.  If you are retrofitting your backyard area and running cable is not an option, we can also install wireless access to HDMI or stream TV through a wireless internet media box like Apple TV or Roku.  There really is no situation too challenging for our team!

Having Fun

Outdoor TVs bring a new level of entertainment to the backyard, especially with the varieties of TVs available on the market today. Sizes vary from 32” to 65”, or you could opt for the largest TV in the world at 201”.  Whatever your ideal for outdoor living includes, we can help you make it reality.

]]> Home technology is constantly changing.  Designing entertainment spaces for homes in south Florida is one of our specialties.  And when it comes to consuming media in your home, on TV’s, whole-house music systems, or through your iPad, things are changing.

Multi-media technology that you will find in many homes includes streaming movies on-demand, storing music in the “cloud” or watching TV shows stored on the DVR or TiVo.  But this begs the question of what is the future of media and home technology?  Will you need a Blu-ray player or an Xbox to watch movies?  How safe are the various cloud services?

Well, the cloud is the future.  Our grandkids will marvel (or laugh) at the shiny silver discs we have for music and movies and wonder how we used them…or for what?

Our advice:  Don’t get rid of your Blu-ray player. There are still many factors that make buying or renting a Blu-ray disc the best option for movies, at least for today. But the cloud-based world is on its way.

Quality.  The clarity of a 1080p Blu-ray disc can’t be replicated through any streaming service.  And if you enjoy 3-D, Blu-ray is the way to go.  Streaming media quality from any service is several levels below a CD or Blu-ray.  Same applies to music.  If you want to experience the depth and details of the music, a CD or LP will serve you better than any music streaming service.

Availability. When it comes to movies, the most dependable way to see the most recently released movies is on disc.  Streaming services like Netflix, Amazon and Vudo have tons of content, but nothing recently released.

Safety.  How trustworthy are the cloud services?  What happens if the internet is disabled?  How will you access all the media you had purchased and stored?  And most of the cloud-based services have all of your account information, in hopes that you will buy more media.  How safe is your information?  We know major banks and organizations can sometimes have security breaches, so can the cloud system.

The pros for the cloud are cost and convenience.  These attributes do win out when it comes to streaming and cloud storage for your media.   Our advice to clients is to use the equipment and systems that are best for your home and lifestyle.  If you have multiple homes throughout the world, cloud storage for your music is a great convenience because you can access your music collection anywhere.  And streaming music from Pandora or Spotify is as cost effective as it gets: free.  So for the time being, a mix of cloud based systems and equipment like Blu-ray players and other home entertainment equipment make for the best set up.

These are the TV’s that are leading the way to the future in terms of amazing picture quality and interactive technology. So if you are planning a TV purchase in 2012 – wait until fall to truly get the best of the best.

“These TV’s are the way of the future, well, the immediate future. Both companies won “Best of CES 2012,” and for good reason. They were not only the most talked about technology at CES, but the innovations in TV technology will truly change the way we use them in our home.

Samsung 55" OLED HDTV

The Samsung Super OLED HDTV 55” supports active-shutter 3D and has smart interaction technology that allows control via remote, voice, motion, and even face recognition. There are no confirmed release dates, but Samsung expects them to go to market by summer 2012.


If you can’t see the video click here.

LG 55 inch OLED HDTV

The LG Display OLED 55” HDTV doesn’t include all the smart technology but received raves about its design, including an incredibly thin body of 4 mm thick and a total weight of 16 pounds. The release date has been set for July 2012. The price is unknown, although DisplaySearch estimates the initial cost at $8,000 and dropping to $4,000 in 2013.

Why OLED?
OLED means Organic Light Emitting Diode. This is new TV technology that doesn’t need backlighting to produce a picture, so the screens can be super thin. This could lead to screens in the future that can roll up – that thin. But what this new technology really means to you the consumer is improved picture quality of 1,000,000:1 contrast. This means the contrast colors are more vivid – the whites are whiter and the black is blacker. Because OLED doesn’t use backlighting like LCD or LED TV’s, the black levels can be higher, more similar to what you experience at a movie theater. There is also improved color pop and viewing angles are increased.

The drawbacks are that this is a new technology and the life span of the TV is expected to be a few years less than a LCD or LED TV (10 years of viewing 4 hours a day, everyday versus 20 years of viewing 4 hours a day). This technology will improve over time, but early adopters may experience some minor challenges.
